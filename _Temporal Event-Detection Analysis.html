Temporal Event-Detection Analysis¶

Saunders Cox¶

Goal:¶

Identify how sick Twitter users from Blacksburg get during the transition from the end of Summer into the beginning of Autumn. Using keywords that users use such as "sick", "fever", and "flu" that were used on various days during the time period covered by the data using a vector space model matrix using TFDIF, we can estimate a trend. Perhaps we can even foresee when students, athletes, and workers of Blacksburg should take the most precautions to stay healthy throughout flu season. We can assume that not all keywords mentioned will be generated due to an actual ailment. However we can hypothesize that the words will pop up more following August and perhaps continue onward through October.

Results and Conclusion¶

What can we gather from the Weekly TF graphs?¶

There were no trends that looked at any specific day of the week that the terms came up. We can very clearly see how it is more common for a tweet on any given day to mention the word "sick" than it is to mention "fever" or "flu", likely because it is more commmon for someone to become sick then to become severely ill. Although a getting a fever may be less common than getting sick and more common than getting the flu, it isn't mentioned all too much because few people mention fever. Another concept to consider is that the word sick can mean more than one dimension of sentiment i.e. sick can indicate a positive response, while a fever rarely indicates something positive. The flu could also be mentioned more frequently due to promotions of flu shots around the Blacksburg area.

What can we gather from the 3 Month TFIDF graph?¶

Firstly, we can see that the term "sick" tends to oscillate up and down. What seems to be a common case is that the TFIDF will ramp up and then die out. This could indicate that since it starts out very low, that it is frequently mentioned abruptly, and then the "sickness" tapers off.

Secondly, we can see that the term "flu" tends to become very dense but naturally much less common than "sick" around late September. This could be due to the promotion of flu shots, but perhaps some are due to people actually tweeting about enduring the flu. Going into October, we see much fewer days with the flu being mentioned, but since the TFIDF is lower, it could be mentioned multiple times within those sporadic October days.

Lastly, the term "fever" doesn't seem to come up reliably as the other words, but we can see that when it does come up, there is undubitably talk about the terms "sick" and "flu". It would seem less likely for someone to mention "fever" without referring to themselves, so we this term could help us understand when people are actually suffering from a seasonal sickness. After the first wave of "fever" passes, it doesn't come up again until a couple weeks later, but then seems to die out again. Could the fever come in waves? It would make sense that if something is contagious in the town is going around, that more people would talk about it in close succession.
