Comparisons of IMDb Movie Review Attributes¶

Saunders Cox¶

What are the relationships between review sentiment, review length, and rating of reviews?

We know that ratings are an indicator of someone's perception of a movie, but can we make any assumption about the review's length or sentiment depending on the rating?

We know that the reviews have hard defined ratings and review length; however the review sentiment is fairly subjective given that we are using a predefined set of positive and negative words to estimate the net sentiment (positive words - negative words).

The following movies were analyzed:

  1. Silence of the Lambs
  2. The Shining
  3. La La Land
  4. Moulin Rouge
  5. Shrek
  6. Finding Nemo
  7. Dark Skies
  8. Paprika
  9. The Conjuring 2
  10. Fight Club

We have mystery, horror, drama, comedy, family, and thriller genres to gather a diverse set of language. It should seem that given:

  • Low rating -> Mix of low and high length reviews
    • I expect a reviewer to not give a movie they disliked much thought, or perhaps they had much to complain about.
  • Low rating -> Low net sentiment
    • I expect the negative language to overpower the positive language; however there may be some positive language that is simply a counterpoint to what the movie could have been.
  • High rating -> Generally higher length reviews
    • I expect praise to translate into longer discussion of the movie.
  • High rating -> High net sentiment
    • I expect the higher rated reviews to translate into mostly positive sentiment. However for the horror films, the negative language may be stronger and cancel out some of the positive language. This would produce some misinterpretation of the reviews, but I would expect on average, the positive sentiment is greater.

Q 1.1 Rating vs Length¶

As mentioned, the rating of a review can be measured on a very narrow scale in comparison to the other measuring methods. What trend might we expect to see when we compare the most quantitative values: rating on a scale of 1-10 and length of the review? It would make sense that people would have more to say the more polarized their feelings are towards the movie. Let's see if that holds up.

Q 1.3 Rating vs Length Conclusion¶

It appears that the lower quartiles hover around the same area. These are reviews that had little effort; the users probably wanted to get their star rating submitted and be done with the review.

On the other hand, we can see that the upper quartiles vary much more greatly. There is no clear trend for the lower ratings, but on the ratings for 7 and up, we can assume that users generally had more to discuss. This seems to support one of our hypotheses--that higher rating translates to longer reviews.

There seems to be more outliers on either end of the spectrum, but that is simply because in the figure following this cell, it is shown that there are far more reviewers that give 1 star and 10 star reviews.

Q 1.3 Cont.¶

Although there was a clear correlation to reviewer count to ratings, there was no apparent correlation to the average z-score review length per rating across the board.

However, we can guess that people that give low scores have less to say on average. While the smaller crowd who gives a movie a rating of 3 to 5 have a bit more reasoning on their rating. And for some reason, people who give a 6 generally won't say as much. And people who give a 7 to a 10 will write a lengthy review with the odd exception of people who give an 8.

This information is important to reveal upfront to better understand how the rest of our data will be analyzed. The gist of this precursor is to understand that most reviews will be polarized, and that the average length of each review has a strong correlation with rating only on polarized ratings i.e. movies with the lowest ratings have lower length reviews, while movies with the highest ratings have higher length reviews, and movies with ratings towards the middle have no clear correlation on review length.

Q 2.1 Rating vs. Net Sentiment¶

Now we have a more abstract measurement of sentiment. It might seem obvious to make an assumption that rating will have a positive correlation to what we will refer to as "net sentiment". But is that the case? Remember that our movie selection is diverse, so we'll have a good mix of all reviews, so if we find some oddities in our visualizations, we can theorize what's happening.

Let's first define how we are measuring sentiment. We will count how many positive words, negative words, and total words we have in each review and use them to analyze our data.

Q 2.2 Expanding Our Data¶

To evaluate sentiment we'll need to dig deeper to create more data from our data. We'll begin by storing all the positive words and negative words and the amount of each to the main dataframe.

data['NegCount'] = [len(data.Negatives[i]) for i in range(data.shape[0])]

data['NetSentiment'] = [(data.iloc[i].PosCount - data.iloc[i].NegCount) for i in range(0, data.shape[0])]

Q 2.2 Cont. Net Sentiment vs Rating¶

(Scatterplot as a Variation of Boxplot)¶

Now we can use combine some of the data we generated from a sentiment analysis.

First we'll use a partially discrete scatter plot. Although it may seem intuitive to use a bar graph or a histogram for everything when using discrete measurements such as rating, we can more easily visualize multiple dimensions with a scatterplot with varying color and sizes.

If we have a scatter plot with the discrete rating on the X-axis, and the net sentiment on the Y-axis, we can see that the majority of reviews result in a net sentiment between -10 and 10 at rating 1. That range gradually seems to condense but not change too much up to rating 5. And from there onward to rating 10, the net sentiment tends to broaden from -5 to 20.

Q 2.3 Conclusion of Net Sentiment vs Rating¶

From the original scatterplot, we can visualize an ever so slight upward trend towards more positive language as ratings increase. However, it becomes much more clear in the second scatterplot that net sentiment isn't as clear of a visualization as simply mapping negative language against positive language where each point is colored by rating.

It's interesting to see that there is a mix of both positive and negative language, but it is very clear that there is more negative language associated with poor reviews, and more positive language associated with glowing reviews.

And finally we display our top uses of "positive" and "negative" language.

"Good", "Great", "Love", "Perfect", "Brilliant", and "Fun" seem to be good indicators of a glowing review. On the other side, words such as "Just", "Will", and "Even" seem to be very generic words that inflated the positive measurement of the reviews counting for more than 2000 positive words.

For the "negative" language, we have good indicators of a poor review with words such as "Bad", "Boring", and "Worst". On the other hand, we have words that may glorify a movie with sinister themes such as "Horror", "Scary", "Killer", and "Dark", and thus unjustly deflate the praise a reviewer has given to a movie.

In the end, the correlation somewhat supported our hypothesis. If, however, the reviews were for a hotel experience, or a restaurant, it would seem that the correlation of negative and positive words towards negative and positive reviews would be stronger.
