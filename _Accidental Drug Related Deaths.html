Overview¶

Over the past century, drug abuse in our nation has sky rocketed. What are the trends, and what can we infer from the data? We know there has been a synthetic opioid crisis permeating the United States. Are there any clues or patterns we can gather given drug data from a specific state? Could we isolate an outlier to show us what may be happening in the drug market? With this data, no hard conclusions can be made, but perhaps it will reveal some parallels to recent reports from various news outlets.

Data¶

What is our data?¶

In this analysis, we will be looking at a report of accidental drug related deaths in Connecticut. We are choosing this set of data, because it contains some of the most recent information on the subject that may reflect a trend in the overall drug market. This data set is not representative of the population of the U.S., because drug popularity varies significantly from region to region. However, a significant change over the course of 6 years may reveal something less obvious.

The data we are using was updated on May 8, 2019 from https://catalog.data.gov/dataset/accidental-drug-related-deaths-january-2012-sept-2015 by the Office of the Chief Medical Examiner.

Primary Culprits¶

Can we conclude that a drug crisis is effectively being handled if the total amount of incidents is going down? Let's examine the primary culprits. As shown, the top two leading causes of accidental deaths stem from the opioids: heroin, made from morphine, a natural substance harvested from poppy seeds, and fentanyl, a synthetic opioid similar to morphine but much more potent and likely to be mixed with either heroin or cocaine.

The following visualization demonstrates this, but it is important to understand that multiple drugs could have accounted for a single death. For example, if both heroin and fentanyl were related to a death, they would be counted once for each drug.

Years vs. Drug¶

Among the top five drugs associated with accidental deaths, four of the drugs were less likely to cause death in 2018 than the previous year, 2017. Could that be because they were used less, or could it link to rapidly rising fentanyl on the black market?

According to an article on MDEdge Psychiatry Richard Franki, "The [U.S. data shows] that total drug overdose deaths were down from 70,699 in 2017 to 67,744 in 2018, a drop of 4.2%, the CDC said." Although the data varies throughout the U.S., Connecticut's mild drop in drug related deaths seems to be a good representative of the overall trend in the U.S. The article also quotes that in 2018, "...the number of patients receiving medication-assisted treatment has risen, distribution of overdose-reversing drugs is up, and nationwide opioid prescriptions are down.

Source: https://www.mdedge.com/psychiatry/article/204863/addiction-medicine/drug-overdose-deaths-declined-2018

There seems to be no significant trend among heroin, cocaine, benzodiapene, and ethanol, except that they are all known to be used together. It is also known that fentanyl is commonly used with other drugs. It seems that fentanyl's rise, although increasing at a lower rate than in previous years, is still increasing at a significant enough rate to remind us that the opioid crisis is not fully resolved.

Narrowing Our Focus on Fentanyl¶

Here are some of the things we learned from our initial analysis.

  • The number of deaths associated with drugs sorted from most to least show that the opioids are the leading causes of accidental deaths:
    • Heroin

Fentanyl Related Deaths by Month¶

We further examine Fentanyl related deaths in more detail by viewing the trend by each month. We can't necessarily say we have a success when the death count decreases by a single month, because the change in death count varies tremendously from month to month. What we can hypothesize is that there may be an interval associated with death spikes. The spike isn't a constant interval, but we can estimate that about every 2 to 5 months there is another dramatic increase in the number of deaths.

Conclusion & Questions¶

As we've analyzed this data, we can see that Fentanyl abuse is being addressed, but shows no sure trend of a steady decline. One interesting trend we saw in the data was the interval of increase and decrease of deaths varying among a few months at a time. We've speculated that the peaks and valleys of deaths in certain months may be related to a way the drug market functions. We could also speculate that government's promotion of medication-assisted treatment are released on some interval. It seems that the growth of accidental drug related deaths has leveled off across the board, but we'll have to analyze the future year's report to get a sense of how effective the resolution of the drug crisis will be.
